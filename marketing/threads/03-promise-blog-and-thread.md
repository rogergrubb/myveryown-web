### "I built an AI wedding companion. Here's what it can't do — and why I'm shipping it anyway."

I've watched four people plan weddings in the last three years. They didn't need help finding a venue. They needed help remembering what they decided about the venue, what their mother said about the venue, and what their fiancé's reaction was when they brought the venue up at dinner.

The thing that wears couples down isn't the planning. It's the **same conversation, six times, over twelve months, with seven different people**.

That's the gap I built The Promise for.

#### What it does

The Promise is one of twenty AI personas in a product called My Very Own. Each persona has a domain — fitness coach, faith companion, K-pop bestie, pet loss support. The Promise is the wedding planning companion. They share one memory of you across all twenty.

So when you say to The Promise:

> "Mom said she's not coming if my brother's plus-one is invited."

It writes that down.

When you say, three weeks later:

> "Decided we're doing the welcome dinner Friday."

It remembers Mom's pre-condition. It might gently ask whether the guest list situation got resolved.

When the time comes to book a vendor and you forget what your fiancé thought about Photographer A vs. Photographer B because that conversation was a month ago and you've talked to 14 people since — you ask The Promise. It tells you what you actually said to them, not what you remember saying.

#### What it does NOT do

It doesn't pick your venue. It doesn't write your vows. It doesn't reach out to vendors. It doesn't reduce the actual emotional load of merging two families. It doesn't replace your planner if you have one. It doesn't promise the wedding will go smoothly.

It can't make your mom okay with the budget. It can't keep your sister from making the toast political. It can't pick a song that makes everyone cry the right way.

It's a memory layer. It's not a planner.

#### Why I'm telling you what it can't do

Because the AI wedding planning space is full of products promising to "automate your wedding". They can't, and shouldn't.

Wedding planning is one of the few remaining occasions in adult life where the friction itself is the point. The conversations about who-sits-where and when-do-we-tell-grandma-about-the-non-religious-officiant are how families actually merge. Outsourcing those conversations to AI defeats the purpose.

What I built is more boring than that. It just remembers. So when you have the same conversation with your fiancé for the third time, you're at least having it from the same place.

#### The thing I didn't expect

Couples in our test group used The Promise mostly for **family conversation memory** — not budget tracking, not vendor coordination. They wanted to remember:

- What they'd told whose parent
- Which traditions they'd agreed to bend
- Why they decided to include or exclude a particular cousin
- The actual reasoning behind decisions, not just the decisions

The budget tracker spreadsheet exists. Vendor checklists exist. What didn't exist before The Promise: a place to remember the **conversations**.

#### How to try it

It's free for seven days. No credit card. You can stop using it whenever; nothing locks in.

If you try it and it sucks, tell me specifically why. That's how I'll improve it.

## NOTES FOR ROGER

- **The blog post is the trust artifact.** It's deliberately not a sales page. The "what it can't do" section needs to read as honest, not fake-modest. Re-read it before publishing — make sure your voice is in the words, not mine.
- **Don't post the blog and the thread the same day.** Post the blog Monday morning (10 AM ET). Post the thread Tuesday morning (9 AM PT). The thread linking back to the blog drives the right kind of click — readers committed to reading > readers who skim.
- **Reply policy on the thread**: anyone who shares their own wedding-planning frustration story, reply with empathy first, product mention second (or not at all). Wedding audience converts on perceived emotional intelligence, not feature lists.
- **DO NOT mention competitors.** "Other wedding planning AIs" is fine; naming Pinterest, The Knot, or any specific app is not.
- **Cultural sensitivity**: this thread skews toward American Western weddings. If you want to expand to Indian weddings (huge wedding market, very different friction patterns), that's a separate piece — DON'T retrofit this one.

## REPLY HOOKS WORTH WATCHING FOR

- "Does it remember vendor convos?" → Yes, just paste them in. The Promise has full memory of anything you tell it.
- "Is it giving you advice or just listening?" → Mostly listening, asks questions when you're stuck. Doesn't push opinions on family politics — those are your call.
- "What about Hindu/Jewish/queer weddings?" → The persona doesn't have built-in cultural assumptions. Tell it the traditions, it'll remember them.
- "How is this different from Notion?" → Notion is a database. The Promise is a conversation. Different muscle.

## METRICS TO WATCH

- Click-through from blog → `/start/promise`. Wedding audience is slower; expect 48-72h delay before signups peak.
- 14-day retention is the real signal here. Wedding audience using it once = curious. Using it weekly = real product-market fit for that persona.
- DMs to your account from people who say "tried it, here's my feedback". Wedding planners are blunt; their feedback is gold.
